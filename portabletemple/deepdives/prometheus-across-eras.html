Hesiod, Aeschylus, Shelley: one name, three eras

The clearest case of a myth being rewritten to fit the politics of its moment. Same character, three completely different verdicts, each matching the century that told it.

On Shelley specifically

Prometheus Unbound (1820) is built on the Greek myth, but rewrites the ending: Prometheus is eventually freed (by the figure Demogorgon), and Zeus is overthrown. It's a complex, allegorical exploration of freedom, love, and the power of imagination — and a fairly direct indictment of tyranny, written in the shadow of the French Revolution and the Napoleonic Wars, while Shelley himself, a radical who'd been expelled from Oxford for a pamphlet critical of religion, believed strongly in the power of imagination to drive social change. He saw Prometheus as a symbol of the human spirit. The myth hadn't changed; the century reading it had.

The same instinct — give the old story a new ending that suits the moment — recurs much later: Ursula K. Le Guin's short story "Prometheus Rising" frees Prometheus through the intervention of a group of humans who have been inspired by him, a modern variant of the same move Shelley made two centuries earlier.
